The Doppler Effect

When a source of waves moves relative to an observer, the observed frequency differs from the emitted frequency. A source approaching the observer compresses wavefronts — raising the perceived pitch. A receding source stretches them — lowering the pitch.

This applies to all wave types: sound, light, radar, ultrasound, and gravitational waves. The effect is fundamental to astrophysics, medical imaging, weather radar, and everyday experience (a passing ambulance siren).

This simulation models the exact Doppler formula with both moving source and moving observer, visualizes the expanding circular wavefronts, and draws the Mach cone when the source exceeds the wave speed.

Historical Background

1842
Christian Doppler published Über das farbige Licht der Doppelsterne, predicting that the color of a star should change depending on whether it approaches or recedes — the first mathematical treatment of what is now called the Doppler effect.
1845
Christoph Buys Ballot tested Doppler's prediction for sound by having musicians ride a train while playing a note — observers on the platform confirmed the predicted pitch shift, the first experimental verification.
1848
Hippolyte Fizeau independently derived the effect for electromagnetic waves and noted that spectral line shifts could measure stellar velocities — the basis of modern radial velocity astronomy.
1887
Ernst Mach photographed the shock wave cone produced by a supersonic bullet, providing the first visual evidence of the "Mach cone" — the acoustic shock wave created when a source exceeds the wave speed.
1929
Edwin Hubble used the Doppler shift of galaxy spectra to discover that galaxies recede at velocities proportional to their distance — Hubble's Law — establishing the expanding universe.
1950s
Doppler radar was developed for weather forecasting and aviation, measuring the frequency shift of reflected radio waves to determine wind and precipitation velocities.
1960s
Medical Doppler ultrasound was developed, using the frequency shift of reflected sound to measure blood-flow velocities non-invasively — now a routine diagnostic tool.

Key quantities:

  • fs — emitted frequency of the source
  • vs — source velocity (positive = rightward)
  • vo — observer velocity (positive = rightward)
  • v — wave propagation speed in the medium
  • fo — observed frequency

Important: The Doppler effect for sound depends on velocities relative to the medium (air), not just relative to each other. This distinguishes it from the relativistic Doppler effect for light.

Derivation

Moving Source, Stationary Observer

When the source moves at speed vs toward the observer, it chases its own wavefronts. The wavelength ahead of the source is compressed:

λahead = (v − vs) / fs

The observer encounters these compressed wavefronts at speed v, so the observed frequency is:

fo = v / λahead = fs · v / (v − vs)

Behind the source the wavefronts are stretched, giving:

fo,behind = fs · v / (v + vs)

Moving Observer

When the observer moves toward the source at speed vo, they encounter wavefronts at a higher rate — their effective approach speed to each wavefront is (v + vo):

fo = fs · (v + vo) / v

General Formula

Combining both moving source and observer:

fo = fs · (v + vo) / (v − vs)
vs: + when source moves toward observer
vo: + when observer moves toward source
v: wave speed in medium (always positive)

The upper signs apply when the relevant party moves toward the other; lower signs when moving away.

Mach Number & Shock Waves

Mach Number

The Mach number M is the ratio of source speed to wave speed:

M = vs / v
M < 1: subsonic · M = 1: sonic · M > 1: supersonic

Sonic Singularity (M = 1)

When M = 1, the source keeps pace with its own wavefronts. All wavefronts pile up directly ahead of the source, creating an infinitely compressed wavefront. The formula gives fo → ∞ directly ahead — a singularity.

Supersonic — Mach Cone (M > 1)

When M > 1, the source outruns its wavefronts. The wavefronts form a conical envelope — the Mach cone or shock wave. The half-angle θ of the cone is:

sin θ = v / vs = 1 / M
θ decreases as M increases; narrow cone = very supersonic

The observer hears nothing until the shock wave passes — then experiences a sudden pressure jump (a "sonic boom" for aircraft). Behind the Mach cone, the source has already passed and the observed frequency is:

fo,behind = fs · v / (v + vs)

Key asymmetry: A source approaching at 200 m/s (in 343 m/s air) gives a different frequency shift than an observer approaching at 200 m/s — the formula is not symmetric. This is because the Doppler formula involves velocities relative to the medium, not each other.

Guided Experiments

1. Classic Pitch Shift

Set fs=440 Hz, v=343 m/s. Increase source velocity to 100 m/s. Compare "f ahead" vs "f behind" in the graph. The pitch ahead rises to ~577 Hz; behind it drops to ~347 Hz. This is the ambulance/train effect.

2. Sonic Singularity

Slowly increase vs toward 343 m/s (matching the wave speed). Watch "f ahead" climb rapidly toward infinity as M → 1. At exactly M=1 the formula breaks down — the source outruns its own wavefronts.

3. Mach Cone

Set vs = 500 m/s (M ≈ 1.46 in 343 m/s air). A red dashed Mach cone appears. Increase vs further — the cone angle θ = arcsin(1/M) becomes narrower. Verify: at vs=686 m/s (M=2), θ = 30°.

4. Source vs. Observer Asymmetry

Set v=343 m/s. Compare: (a) vs=100 m/s, vo=0 vs. (b) vs=0, vo=100 m/s. The observed frequencies differ — proving the formula is not symmetric in source and observer velocity.

5. Wavefront Compression Visualization

Enable wavefronts, set vs=150 m/s. Watch the rings bunch up ahead of the source and stretch out behind. The spacing between rings (λobs) is proportional to 1/fobs — verify visually.

6. Observer Motion

Set all velocities to zero. Set vo=200 m/s (observer moving toward stationary source). Observe the frequency rise. Now try vs=200 m/s (source approaching stationary observer). Same relative speed — different frequency due to the asymmetry of sound Doppler.
